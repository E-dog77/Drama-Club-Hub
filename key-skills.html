Key Skills in Drama

Presence

The ability to command attention on stage through energy, focus, and awareness. A strong stage presence draws the audience in, even when a character is silent.

Voice Control

Using your voice intentionally—adjusting pitch, volume, tone, and breath—to suit a character, emotion, or dramatic moment.

Body Language

Communicating emotion, status, and intention through physicality. This includes posture, gestures, and movement.

Emotional Range

The ability to authentically express a wide variety of emotions, from subtle to extreme, while staying connected to character.

Timing and Rhythm

Mastering the pace of your delivery and movement, especially for comedic or dramatic effect. Good timing keeps scenes alive and engaging.

Listening and Reacting

Being truly present in a scene by paying attention to scene partners and responding honestly, not just waiting to speak.

Memorisation and Recall

Learning lines, blocking, or choreography and retrieving them accurately and consistently during performance.

Musicality

Understanding rhythm, pitch, and tempo in performance. This applies to songs, stylised movement, or vocal variety in monologues.

Coordination

The ability to move your body with precision and control, especially in stage combat, choreography, or partner scenes.

Stamina

Sustaining energy and focus during long rehearsals or performances. Physical and mental endurance is vital for consistent work.

Spatial Awareness

Knowing where you and others are on stage. Helps with safe movement, strong blocking, and effective stage pictures.

Discipline

Staying focused, reliable, and committed throughout rehearsals and performance. Includes punctuality, preparation, and respect.

Adaptability

Responding to unexpected changes or mistakes without breaking character. Flexibility in both scripted and improvised work.

Collaboration

Working effectively with others to create theatre. This includes sharing ideas, supporting others, and being open to feedback.

Resilience

Handling feedback, mistakes, nerves, or failure with maturity. The ability to bounce back and try again.

Characterisation

Building and performing a believable character using voice, body, emotion, and imagination.

Imagination

Using creative thinking to invent characters, worlds, or solutions during improvisation and rehearsal.

Expressiveness

Making strong, clear choices with your face, voice, and body to show emotion and character.

Dramaturgy

Understanding the story, context, themes, and structure of a script or scene. Helps inform performance choices.

Projection

Using breath and vocal technique to make your voice carry clearly across a space without shouting.

Articulation

Speaking clearly so every word is understood. Involves precise mouth and tongue movements and vocal control.

Confidence

Believing in your ability to perform. Confidence helps you take risks, make bold choices, and perform without fear.

Substitution (Uta Hagen)

A technique where you connect a character’s emotional experience to your own life, e.g., imagining who you would grieve if your character is mourning.
