Glossary of Theatre and Drama Terms

Below is a list of common drama and theatre terms used in Drama Club. Knowing these terms helps improve communication, understanding, and performance.

  • Articulation: Speaking clearly so every word is understood.
  • Blocking: Planned movement and positioning of actors on stage.
  • Breaking Character: When an actor steps out of their role, usually by laughing or reacting personally.
  • Characterisation: Creating a believable character through voice, body, and emotion.
  • Cue: A signal for an actor to speak, move, or enter/exit.
  • Duologue: A scene or conversation between two actors.
  • Ensemble: The full group of performers, often working together equally.
  • Fourth Wall: The imaginary barrier between the actor and the audience.
  • Freeze: All actors pause in place, often used to start or end a scene.
  • Gesture: A movement of the body or limb to express an idea or emotion.
  • Improvisation: Performing without a script, creating scenes on the spot.
  • Improvisational Prompt: A word, idea, or line used to spark a spontaneous scene.
  • Mime: Expressing meaning through movement without speaking.
  • Monologue: A long speech by one character.
  • Off-prompt: The opposite side of the stage from prompt-side (typically stage-right).
  • Projection: Using your voice clearly and powerfully to be heard.
  • Prompt-side: The side of the stage where the prompt desk or stage manager is traditionally located (stage-left in most theatres).
  • Rehearsal: Practice sessions for developing a performance before an audience sees it.
  • Script: The written text of a play or performance.
  • Stage Left/Right: Directions from the actor’s perspective when facing the audience.
  • Stage Presence: The charisma or magnetism an actor shows on stage.
  • Subtext: The hidden meaning beneath a character’s spoken words.
  • Tableau: A frozen stage picture that tells a story visually.
  • Upstage/Downstage: Upstage is away from the audience; downstage is toward them.
  • Prompt-side: The side of the stage where the prompt desk or stage manager is traditionally located (stage-left in most theatres).
  • Off-prompt: The opposite side of the stage from prompt-side (typically stage-right).
